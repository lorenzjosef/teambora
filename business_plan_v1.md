# Gezellig — Short Business Plan

**Challenge:** The Lonely City  
**Product:** Gezellig  
**Pilot geography:** Rotterdam, then Amsterdam and The Hague  
**Funding context:** EUR 2M seed funding, 18-month pilot  
**Date:** 2026-05-19

---

## Core Idea

Gezellig is an opt-in activity planner for Dutch municipalities. It helps residents turn free moments into shared activities: coffee, walks, museums, sports, language practice, and small hosted events.

The insight is simple: many people want more everyday connection, but they do not want to join something labelled as a loneliness initiative. The barrier is coordination, not willingness: shared interests, overlapping time, safe public places, and comfortable group size.

Gezellig solves that coordination problem without labelling residents.

> We do not use AI to detect loneliness. We use AI to reduce the coordination friction that prevents real-life connection.

Residents use an alias, choose interests, add free time windows, set comfort preferences, and receive a maximum of three clear suggestions. Each suggestion explains why it appears: "Fits your Thursday evening availability," "Public location within your travel radius," or "Small-group setting matches your comfort choice."

The product feels like a normal city activity planner, not a welfare intake, diagnosis tool, surveillance system, or dating app.

---

## Why It Will Work

### 1. It avoids stigma

Traditional outreach asks people to identify with a problem: loneliness. Many residents will not do that, even when they would welcome more connection.

Gezellig uses a lower-pressure invitation: **Make a free moment social.**

### 2. It reaches people through trusted places

Gezellig is distributed through libraries, cafes, universities, GP waiting rooms, housing associations, sports clubs, museums, language schools, and municipal service desks. Partners invite residents into concrete activities: a coffee table, museum visit, beginner sport, neighborhood walk, or Dutch practice.

### 3. It uses low-friction growth loops

Gezellig reaches new users through normal city touchpoints rather than stigma-based campaigns. The goal is to make joining feel like discovering a local activity, not enrolling in a loneliness program.

Key acquisition channels:

- **QR codes at public venues:** Libraries, cafes, museums, sports centers, GP waiting rooms, universities, municipal desks, community centers, and housing association buildings place QR codes next to concrete activity invitations: "Join a small coffee table Thursday" or "Try a beginner walk nearby."
- **Event-specific QR codes:** Each partner-hosted activity has a QR code on posters, table cards, receipts, newsletters, and venue screens. Scanning opens the exact activity or a short onboarding flow prefilled with that activity category.
- **Member invite links:** Residents can invite someone via a private link after accepting a plan or after a positive meet-up. The link does not expose the sender's profile or private activity history; it only invites the new person into Gezellig or a specific public activity.
- **Bring-one-person prompts:** After a resident gives positive feedback, the app can suggest inviting one friend, neighbor, classmate, colleague, or family member to a future low-pressure activity.
- **Partner newsletters and WhatsApp groups:** Community partners can share public activity links through existing mailing lists, student groups, neighborhood WhatsApp groups, sport clubs, museum programs, and library channels.
- **Municipal service moments:** Residents already interacting with city services can be offered a non-stigmatizing invitation: "Find a local activity this week." This works better than asking people to self-identify as lonely.
- **Ambassador hosts:** Trusted volunteers, librarians, student mentors, sport coaches, language teachers, and community workers can invite residents directly and help first-time users join hosted public events.
- **Offline-to-online follow-up:** Printed cards at events include a QR code to rate the meet-up, meet again, or invite another participant. This turns completed real-world contact into repeat activity and organic referrals.

The growth loop is intentionally privacy-safe: QR scans, invite links, and partner campaigns are measured only in aggregate. Municipalities can see which channels drive accepted activities and repeat meetups, but not who invited whom, who rejected an invite, or private contact details.

### 4. It creates real meetings, not app engagement

Success is not downloads or time spent in the app. Success is repeated real-world contact.

Residents accept an activity, attend in a public or hosted setting, give feedback, and choose whether they would meet again. That repeat-meet signal shows whether a one-time activity is becoming useful social infrastructure.

### 5. It is transparent and safe

The prototype uses rule-based matching: shared interests, overlapping availability, rough neighborhood or travel radius, public-place preference, small-group comfort, hosted-event preference, and feedback.

It does not create a loneliness score. It does not infer mental health status. It does not classify residents as vulnerable or at risk. It does not expose individual user data to municipalities.

Residents stay in control through alias-first onboarding, public-place defaults, small-group options, hosted events, cancel/report/block controls, and clear explanations.

### 6. It gives municipalities useful data without surveillance

Municipalities do not need a list of lonely people. They need to know where connection can be made easier.

Gezellig shows aggregate-only insight: accepted suggestions, completed meetups, repeat meetups, popular activity types, neighborhood engagement, unmet demand, and partner capacity gaps. If Thursday evening walks are oversubscribed, the municipality can fund more hosts or venues where demand already exists.

No names, exact addresses, private messages, rejected suggestions, or individual profiles are shown.

## Business Model

The first customer is a coalition of Dutch municipalities. Gezellig starts as a publicly funded pilot and can become municipal SaaS for licensing, implementation, partner coordination, and impact reporting.

Municipalities pay because Gezellig improves uptake of existing community programs, reaches residents without stigma, avoids surveillance-based AI, and gives councils measurable impact within 18 months.

---

## 18-Month Pilot Target

With EUR 2M, Gezellig funds product development, privacy and AI governance, partner onboarding, outreach, activity microgrants, safety, and independent evaluation.

Targets:

- 20,000 residents reached.
- 6,000 opt-in participants.
- 4,000 accepted activities.
- 2,800 completed meetups.
- 900 repeat-meet signals.
- 100 active community partners.
- 15% increase in residents reporting weekly social contact in participating pilot neighborhoods.

---

## Winning Logic

Gezellig is not a social network and not a loneliness detector. It is civic connection infrastructure.

It works because it changes the question from "Who is lonely?" to "Where can we make connection easier?"

That makes the product more dignified for residents, more useful for municipalities, safer under GDPR and AI Act expectations, and easier to explain to a jury.

**Gezellig: make a free moment social.**
